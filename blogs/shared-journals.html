Shared Journals: The Benefits of Vulnerability in a Community

September 4, 2025

I’ve been journaling for as long as I could write — maybe from the age of four. My first notebook was a small purple one, textured with ribs across the cover. Whenever I felt deeply emotional or hurt, I turned to it. Raised religious, I often began my entries with “Dear God” and ended with “Help me,” as though my journal were a bridge to someone who might understand.

God never answered those journal prayers, and over time, religious trauma led me away from Christianity. But what never changed was the craving: I wanted my journal entries to be heard. In some ways, I longed for a shared journal — something safe yet communal, where vulnerability could meet understanding. Not mocked, not judged, not exposed to the wrong people, but truly seen.

In American culture, journals are usually treated as private keepsakes, kept hidden in drawers or locked with keys. Yet part of me wondered: what if the real power of journaling only emerges when it’s shared? What if journals became less about secrecy, and more about connection?

Interestingly, this idea isn’t new. In Japan, a form of shared journal called kōkan nikki (交換日記) became popular in the 1990s, where friends passed a notebook back and forth, writing to one another through entries. Shared journals are part journaling, part conversation — a way of turning private reflection into a communal experience.

Today, that same spirit is reemerging online, as shared journals create spaces for vulnerability, support, and authentic connection. 6Clique is a new-age shared journaling app that connects self-reflection with a supportive, small community.

What Are Shared Journals?

At their core, shared journals are journals you intentionally make visible to others. Instead of being locked away in a drawer, your thoughts, reflections, and emotions are published in a space where others can read, relate, and respond. Unlike collaboration journals (where multiple people contribute to one notebook), shared journals emphasize individual expression with communal support.

In practice, a shared journal might look like a digital entry where you share a personal reflection, and others leave comments of encouragement, empathy, or connection. The focus isn’t on likes or performance, but on creating a space where vulnerability is met with understanding.

At their best, shared journals bridge the gap between solitude and belonging — letting your inner world be seen, while inviting others to meet you with empathy. Platforms such as 6Clique bring this concept online, combining the reflective nature of journaling with the relational benefits of community.


The Benefits of Shared Journals

At their core, shared journals combine the healing power of writing with the supportive energy of community. Unlike a private diary, where your words live in isolation, a shared journal allows others to witness your story — and in that witnessing, something transformative happens.

1

Emotional Support & Validation

When you share openly, you create space for others to respond with empathy. Comments of encouragement, understanding, or even simple acknowledgment can soften feelings of loneliness and remind you that your struggles are seen and valid. Shared journals offer the kind of emotional support many people silently crave but rarely receive.

2

Building Meaningful Connections

Shared journals can turn vulnerability into friendship. When someone reads your words and says, “I’ve felt that too,” a bond begins to form. These aren’t surface-level likes or fleeting interactions — they’re moments of genuine recognition, often leading to lasting connections built on honesty and trust.

3

Accountability and Growth

Writing with the knowledge that others may read your reflections encourages consistency. Over time, you’ll not only see your own growth more clearly, but you’ll also have others reflecting it back to you. That accountability can be a powerful motivator to keep showing up for yourself and your healing journey.

4

Inspiring Others

Your shared vulnerability might be the very thing someone else needs to keep going. By writing honestly, you offer others permission to do the same. Shared journals ripple outward: your courage to be open can inspire others to embrace their own stories and break cycles of silence.


Why Vulnerability Matters in Journaling

Brené Brown, a researcher best known for her work on courage, shame, and vulnerability, defines vulnerability as “uncertainty, risk, and emotional exposure” (Brown, 2021). At first glance, those sound like things we should avoid — yet Brown’s research shows that vulnerability is also the birthplace of connection, creativity, and authentic living.

When applied to journaling, vulnerability is what transforms writing from simple record-keeping into deep self-discovery. A private journal lets you admit truths you might hide from the world, but a shared journal asks you to take it one step further: to risk being seen in your raw, unfiltered humanity.

Why does this matter? Because vulnerability builds bridges. When someone reads your words and relates to them, it creates a moment of resonance — the recognition that “I’m not the only one.” Brown argues that this kind of shared vulnerability is essential for genuine belonging (Brown, 2017). Shared journaling provides a structured, safe way to practice it.

By leaning into vulnerability through journaling — and especially through shared journals — you give yourself permission to show up as you are. That openness not only deepens your relationship with yourself, but also lays the foundation for more authentic, meaningful connections with others.
