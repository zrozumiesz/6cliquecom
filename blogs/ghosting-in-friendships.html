Ghosting in Online Friendships: Finding Meaningful Connection after Loss

September 10, 2025

The closest friendship I ever had wasn’t in real life. It was online. That might sound “pathetic” to some people, but online friendships can hold a kind of emotional honesty that’s impossible to find elsewhere. Online friends don’t care about how you look or what you own—they care about your thoughts, your words, the rawest version of you.

That’s why ghosting in online friendships can feel so devastating. When someone disappears, it’s not just a lost username on a screen. It’s a piece of your heart gone missing.

My online friend’s name was Evelyn. I was 10 years old; she was 12. We met in 2011 over spring break on a writing community called StoryWrite, where I had posted a horror competition. I hadn’t even read her submission yet when she messaged me—half annoyed, half joking—demanding to know why I’d skipped her story.

Her story was called Cold Light, an excerpt from her novel Sunflower. It told the story of a girl trapped in a cold, dark room, vomiting her guts out, suffering at the hands of a half-human, half-beast monster. Evelyn’s writing was beautiful: raw, vivid, and haunting. As an isolated, awkward 10-year-old girl with no friends at school, surviving a rough childhood, her story hit a nerve. It unlocked emotions I kept locked away inside.

We became inseparable. I stole my dad’s old work laptop so I could sneak onto Facebook to message her late at night. Sometimes we talked until 4 in the morning. We shared everything: every thought, every feeling, every experience. School, rage, self-hatred, gossip, family, books, music, our writings—nothing was off limits. For the time I knew her, no one else mattered in the world but the two of us.

Six months later, she disappeared. She left me nothing but an eerie last email:

    “I’m sort of… dead. You might not hear from me again.”

It's been 14 years since she vanished, and I still don’t know what happened to her. It's been 14 years, and I still grieve the online friendship I lost.

I understand the ache of ghosting in online friendships—the kind that leaves you grieving someone who was real to you, even if the rest of the world would say otherwise. Losing an online friend can feel even more painful than losing someone in “real life,” because the connection was so undeniably real.

Why Did My Online Friend Ghost Me?

Many of us log on expecting the familiar ping of a message, a joke, a shared story—but they’re gone. No explanation. No goodbye. Just silence.

Sometimes, we’ll never know why.

Ghosting in online friendships is often a puzzle without answers. Life can pull people away abruptly—family emergencies, new responsibilities, mental health struggles, or a move across the country. Some vanish because they’re overwhelmed by the intensity of connection or feel unworthy of intimacy. Others leave to escape conflict, shame, or guilt, even when it has nothing to do with us. And then there are the inexplicable disappearances—the ones that make us question reality, like someone erased a part of our world without warning.

When my closest online friend Evelyn simply vanished that night, leaving her final haunting email, I never knew what she meant, why she left, or whether she was safe. Her disappearance didn’t just break a friendship; it left a scar that lingered for over a decade.

A reminder that some losses carry no closure.

Ghosting can happen in any online friendship, but it cuts especially deep when the bond was real, emotional, and unfiltered. When someone saw us, truly saw us, and then left without a trace, the grief is not abstract—it’s visceral.


The Emotional Impact of Ghosting in Online Friendships

Ghosting in online friendships doesn’t just leave a void. It leaves a wound. But it’s a wound that’s invisible to other people. Because online friendships don’t fit everyone’s definition of a real friendship, the pain is often dismissed:

    “It’s just some random person online. Who cares?”

But those of us who’ve experienced it know that’s not true. When we pour our thoughts, our fears, our laughter, and our hearts into someone who suddenly disappears, it can feel like the world itself has betrayed us. The silence echoes. Every memory, every late-night conversation, every shared secret becomes a ghost of its own.

We're left broken down with confusion, self-doubt, and grief. Did I do something wrong? Was I not enough? Did I misread our connection? The answers often don’t exist, and the uncertainty intensifies the ache.

Online friendships can be intensely intimate because they strip away the superficialities. There’s no judgment for how we look, no pressure to perform, just raw honesty and emotional exchange. Losing someone who saw us so fully can feel like our hearts were ripped out—a sense of presence and understanding that isn’t easily replaced.

The isolation doesn’t stop there. Many people around us may never understand the depth of the pain. They don’t see the hours we spent laughing, crying, and opening our hearts. They don’t see the invisible bond that, for a moment, made the world feel less lonely. We’re left grieving someone who was real to us, even if the rest of the world would say otherwise.


How to Heal from Ghosting in Online Friendships

Healing from ghosting is never simple, especially when the connection felt irreplaceable. The first step is giving ourselves permission to feel. Grief, confusion, anger, even shame—all of it is valid. The fact that others might dismiss our pain doesn’t make it any less real.

Journaling can be a lifeline. Writing out our emotions—whether typed, handwritten, or spoken aloud—helps untangle the mess of emotions that ghosting leaves behind. Some people find shared journaling especially powerful: sharing our thoughts and feelings with others who understand our experiences can make the invisible grief feel seen and validated, and remind us that we’re not alone.

Talking to supportive friends or communities who understand online bonds is also crucial. Finding people who take our grief seriously, who know that online friendships can be as meaningful as in-person ones, can help make the isolation lift, if only a little.

Sometimes, healing means accepting uncertainty. We may never know why someone left, and that lack of closure is painful. But in that uncertainty, we can reclaim our own narrative: recognizing that our worth, our capacity for connection, and our emotional depth are not defined by whether someone stayed.

Finally, healing can begin by seeking spaces designed for genuine connection. Spaces where vulnerability is safe, where our thoughts and feelings are respected, and where friendships aren’t measured in likes or superficial swipes. These environments help us rebuild trust in others, and in ourselves, after the abrupt absence of a friend who once meant so much.
